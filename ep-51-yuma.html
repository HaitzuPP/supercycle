The core thesis

DCG sent Greg Waisman into the Bittensor ecosystem about two years ago, after Barry Silbert's attention was drawn to the network at a DCG Summit. What Waisman found there, and what he presented at a subsequent state-of-Bittensor address, was a framing of the problem that goes beyond subnet economics or token performance.

The argument is as follows: for the first time in history, intelligence itself is being produced at scale by non-human systems. That changes the distribution of power in ways that centralized control makes catastrophic. If the entities that own and control the infrastructure for producing intelligence are also the entities with existing regulatory relationships, compute access, and distribution networks, the outcome is a level of concentration with no historical precedent.

"Intelligence itself is for the first time something moving toward non-human functions. We have to align everybody towards the importance of what we're doing here, and not just for ourselves but for humanity."

Greg Waisman, CEO Yuma / DCG

This is what Waisman means by "captured AI." Not that any individual lab is bad-faith. It is that the structural conditions of centralization produce capture as a default outcome, regardless of intent.

Background: TradeBlock and Axani

Waisman started his career on Wall Street before founding TradeBlock in 2013. TradeBlock built institutional-grade crypto data infrastructure and an OTC trading platform. DCG and CoinDesk acquired it in 2019-2020. The sale was a proof-of-concept for institutional demand for crypto infrastructure.

After TradeBlock, Waisman founded Axani, focused on post-trade infrastructure for crypto markets. The London Stock Exchange acquired Axani in 2024. Two exits in the infrastructure layer of crypto, both to institutions that represent the traditional finance ecosystem trying to absorb crypto rails.

Why this matters

Waisman's thesis on Bittensor is not the thesis of a pure crypto believer. It is the thesis of someone who has watched traditional institutions acquire crypto infrastructure twice. He knows what captured infrastructure looks like from the inside.

Capture defined

In financial markets, capture means that the regulated entity controls the regulator. The pattern repeats across industries: energy, healthcare, telecommunications. The mechanism is the same: regulatory relationships, lobbying, and incumbent distribution networks create barriers that new entrants cannot clear, even with superior technology.

In AI, the capture vectors are compute (a small number of companies own the GPUs needed to train frontier models), data (proprietary datasets accumulated by existing internet platforms), and regulatory relationships (AI governance is being written in dialogue with the labs, not despite them). Open source compresses the model side of the equation. It does not address compute or data or regulatory capture.

Waisman's argument is that Bittensor represents the only current attempt to build AI infrastructure that is structurally resistant to all three capture vectors simultaneously.

Why Bittensor

Bittensor distributes both the production of AI outputs (via mining) and the governance of those outputs (via the subnet architecture and the validator network). No single entity controls the network. No single entity can redirect the incentives. The TAO token creates coordination without requiring trust in a central counterparty.

Yuma, DCG's operating entity inside the network, is focused on building the human coordination layer around that infrastructure: the relationships, the analysis, and the capital allocation that turns a technical network into a functioning ecosystem for real AI development.

The role of crypto

Waisman's framing here is worth noting explicitly. The crypto component of Bittensor, the TAO token, the subnet emission schedules, the economic incentives for mining, these are not the point. They are the mechanism that makes decentralized coordination possible without requiring a trusted intermediary.

This is a materially different framing from most crypto-native arguments about Bittensor. It does not require belief in crypto as an asset class. It requires only that you accept that trustless coordination at scale is useful for building AI infrastructure that is resistant to capture. The token is the implementation detail, not the investment thesis.

The investor view

The episode does not go deep on Yuma's specific financial position or portfolio. What it does establish is the credibility of the operator making the argument. Two infrastructure exits, both to traditional financial institutions, give Waisman a perspective on centralization risk that is grounded in commercial reality rather than ideology.

The state-of-Bittensor presentation that prompted this episode was framed explicitly as an alignment exercise for the most important people in the Bittensor network in one room. Waisman was trying to orient a room of diverse actors around a shared risk model for the moment they are operating in. That is what network builders do when they are serious.
