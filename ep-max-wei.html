The setup

Max is the CEO of Mataco Labs and the founder behind Subnet 44 (Score) on Bittensor. Score is a computer vision subnet. The mission: convert CCTV footage and broadcast video into structured, queryable data at scale, without requiring clients to buy new hardware or GPU infrastructure.

The origin is sports. Max was previously at a data science company, wanted to explore the sports vertical, and left to start his own operation. To extract meaningful data from broadcast content without purchasing rights from TV channels, the team needed computer vision. That constraint became the foundation for the product.

The original sports focus is now secondary. The core architecture transfers to any industry that produces raw video and wants structured intelligence from it. Manufacturing, retail, fuel stations, logistics. The first commercial client is a chocolate manufacturer. The advisor is Brian McDermott, former manager of Reading FC, whose club also has a head of AI.

The deployment problem

The question every enterprise client asks first: do I need to buy compute to run your solution? The answer from most computer vision companies has been yes: buy H100s, one per site, at scale. For a company with 4,000 fuel stations, that is not a viable conversation. The meeting ends.

Mataco's answer is model distillation. They run distillation on state-of-the-art models (including Meta's SAM 3, the leading segmentation model) to extract compressed expert models that run on the client's existing CPUs. No new hardware. The camera infrastructure the client already owns becomes sufficient.

"The biggest breakthrough we went through from a commercial perspective is when we decided to distill vision. We can run those tiny models on site on our clients' CPUs. We don't need them to buy any compute."

Max, CEO Mataco Labs
Technical note

Meta's SAM 3 (Segment Anything Model) is the current state-of-the-art for segmentation and detection across general computer vision tasks. Running it at full scale requires significant GPU compute. Distilled versions trade some accuracy for orders-of-magnitude reduction in inference cost.

Horizontal vs. vertical

The competitive landscape in computer vision is dominated by vertical solutions. Google AutoML Vision is strong at detection. AWS Rekognition handles facial recognition and object detection. These are purpose-built tools for specific tasks.

The gap Mataco is targeting: no company currently offers a horizontal solution where a single camera deployment supports multiple simultaneous use cases. In retail, that means loss prevention, stock monitoring, and marketing attribution from the same hardware and the same camera stream. Switching between use cases today requires switching vendors, redeploying infrastructure, or building in-house.

Building horizontally requires a computer vision team, a DevOps team, GPU procurement for training, and on-site deployment expertise. That combination of capabilities is what Mataco is assembling and packaging as a managed platform, built on top of a continuously improving open-source model library generated by SN44 miners.

PwC and the client profile

PwC is co-selling Mataco's platform. The partnership is live and already generating client introductions. The first named client is a chocolate manufacturer with approximately $2B in annual revenue, to be revealed publicly at Viva Tech Paris in June 2025, where Max will present on stage with the client.

The use case in manufacturing: computer vision identifying product defects, wastage, and quality control issues in real time on the factory floor. Max's framing: tens of tons of chocolate per factory that would otherwise go to waste. Real value, measurable, auditable, not speculative.

90% of current Mataco conversations are with companies above $1B in annual revenue. The enterprise orientation is deliberate: these organizations already own camera infrastructure, have IT budgets, and have existing relationships with PwC that create a warm introduction path. The bottleneck is not demand; it is the deployment friction around compute, which distillation has now addressed.

The role of SN44

Score started on Bittensor to bootstrap R+D. Miners on SN44 train and improve computer vision models by competing on specific vision tasks. Early on, the incentive mechanism was rough: miners gamed it, the team got exploited, and they had to rebuild the scoring function. That is the standard subnet operator journey. The reward comes when the incentive mechanism is good enough for the flywheel to run.

The current relationship between Mataco Labs and SN44 is evolving. As the commercial product matures and the team internalizes more of the model development, the dependency on miners for core R+D decreases. Miners remain valuable for expanding the model library and handling novel task types. But the critical path for enterprise deployment is now largely internal.

Wei, who spent significant time studying reinforcement learning in robotics, made the point that Bittensor's incentive mechanism framework is broadly applicable to any problem that can be formulated as a search or optimization task. Robotics subnets have not yet materialized in meaningful numbers, despite the obvious parallel. Wei attended Proof of Talk in Paris the prior year and discussed the framework with Max in person.

Market context

The episode opens with Venice token approaching $1B market cap, and a copycat token going 5x in one week. Wei's read on the broader market: post-April-lows, risk appetite is returning on-chain. His investment advice, delivered without irony: buy semiconductors, lever 3x, and wait. The S+P was at all-time highs. The sentiment was optimistic.

Max's read on whether the AI market is toppy: no. The value creation is real, not just narrative. Tens of tons of chocolate saved per factory per year is the grounding data point. Capital will follow demonstrable efficiency gains. The concern is speed of selection: investors are moving fast to find winners, and founders who can show real results and generate community attention will attract capital before the window closes.
