Investment Opportunity

Robot hardware has fallen from $1M to under $100k in five years. The ownership infrastructure has not been built.

Industrial robot costs have followed the same curve as solar panels and EV batteries, and the companies building the ownership and coordination infrastructure for robot fleets are 5 years ahead of where the capital is looking.

The Thesis

Why this is investable now

The deflationary pressure on hardware is real. Boston Dynamics, Agility Robotics, Figure, and 1X are all making humanoid robots viable at sub-$100k price points, a cost curve that sat above $1M five years ago. The AI layer, including computer vision, spatial computing, and autonomous decision-making, is now good enough to make general-purpose robots useful in constrained environments. Both curves broke at the same time. That is not a coincidence; it is the window.

The question is not whether robots proliferate. It is who owns them and on what terms. Centralized robot ownership by Amazon, Tesla, and Foxconn means the economic upside of robot labor accrues to equity holders of those companies. Decentralized robot ownership creates a new asset class: productive robot infrastructure that individual investors or collectives can own and deploy. The ownership structure determines who captures the value.

The spatial computing layer is the missing piece. For robots to coordinate in shared physical spaces, they need a persistent, low-latency map of the world. Auki is building that map as shared infrastructure. BitRobot is putting robot ownership on-chain. These are not competing bets; they are complementary infrastructure plays on the same underlying transition. One solves the coordination problem, the other solves the ownership problem.

The Opportunity

What this means for investors

01 / Decentralized robot ownership protocols

The closest analog is real estate investment trusts, but the productive asset is a robot, not a building. BitRobot's on-chain ownership model enables fractional investment in robot fleets that generate revenue from task completion. The total addressable base is every industrial and logistics environment that currently pays human labor for repetitive, structured tasks. That is not a niche.

02 / Spatial computing infrastructure

Auki's shared map is a picks-and-shovels play. Every robot deployment in a shared environment becomes a potential customer. The infrastructure layer earns from usage without needing to own or operate a single robot. As deployment density increases, the network effects of a shared map compound: more robots contribute data, which makes the map more accurate, which attracts more robots. The moat builds itself.

03 / On-chain coordination layers

Protocols that allow robot fleets to bid for tasks, settle payments, and log provenance without a central dispatcher create a new category of infrastructure. The economic model is closer to a marketplace than a SaaS product: take rates on task volume rather than fixed subscription revenue. As robot fleets scale, the marketplace economics become significant without requiring the protocol to own any of the underlying hardware.

04 / Entry timing

The market is pricing humanoid robotics as a 10-year story. The infrastructure enabling that story needs to be built in the next 3 years. Spatial computing networks, ownership protocols, and task coordination layers cannot be built after robots are already deployed at scale; they need to be in place before deployment density makes coordination necessary. That is where the asymmetry sits: the infrastructure is cheap now because the market is not looking at it yet.
