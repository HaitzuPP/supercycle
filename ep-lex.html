The robot money thesis

Generative Ventures launched three years ago, just after the FTX collapse, with a specific framing: economic activity is going to come from robots. Software AI agents, hardware robots, and humans so augmented by agents that their labor persists in an automated fashion. Financial infrastructure for that new class of economic actor is the durable opportunity.

Sokolin, formerly chief economist at Consensys and an early founder in the roboadvice space (FinTech 1.0), saw this from his vantage point across three markets simultaneously: the US, Asia, and the intersection of crypto and traditional finance. His partner is Asia-based, giving the fund a view into what the large labs in different jurisdictions are actually building, not just what they are announcing.

The thesis was early. It pulled teeth to get the fund going in 2022. It is now entirely mainstream.

"These days I call the thesis robot money. Stripe wants to be robot money. Ethereum wants to be robot money. Solana and Visa and MasterCard and BlackRock with its tokenized fund, everybody wants to be robot money."

Lex Sokolin, Managing Partner, Generative Ventures

The consensus problem

The validation of a thesis is also its death as a differentiation strategy. Han Ventures raised $1B for an AI crypto fund targeting the same thesis. Stripe, Coinbase, OpenAI, and Anthropic are all sitting on it. In Asia, Ant Financial and WeChat are already there. The window that existed three years ago is closed or closing very fast.

For a startup, this means the time between identifying a product insight and having that insight commoditized by a large incumbent has compressed dramatically. Sokolin's framing is precise: it is not that the thesis is wrong. It is that being right about a thesis is no longer sufficient to build a company around it.

The implication

If large companies can deploy agentic workflows into existing distribution footprints faster than startups can acquire users, the startup advantage shifts to narrow problems incumbents cannot or will not solve, and to geographies and verticals where existing distribution does not reach.

The three asset class problem

Sokolin tracks approximately 100 companies quarterly across public equities, private equity, and token markets, using Claude for analysis. His read on the current state of each market as of early 2025:

Public equities: Big tech and energy have done well. AI-adjacent infrastructure (chips, memory, compute) has outperformed because it captures the capex cycle of the large labs. Applications-layer companies, particularly SaaS, have been repriced down because AI commoditizes their core value proposition. Adobe generates more revenue than before, at a lower multiple. The gap between revenue performance and market valuation reflects expected disruption, not current disruption.

Private equity: Late-stage has done well. Early-stage has bled out in both token markets and private markets. The capital influx from large tech balance sheets ($100B-$300B in cash being redeployed into AI) went primarily to infrastructure, then to inference providers and labs, not to early-stage applications.

Token markets: Structurally broken at the early stage. No meaningful on-chain capital is systematically evaluating which of 5 million tokens represent real value. TGE-plus-market-maker is widely understood as a mechanism for exiting into retail, not creating returns.

Differentiation compression

The historical VC model assumes that small companies can move faster than large ones. Returns to scale level off; agility is the startup advantage. Sokolin's observation is that this relationship has inverted in the current environment. Large companies with existing distribution, regulatory relationships, and customer data can deploy agentic workflows into their installed base faster than startups can acquire users from scratch.

Stripe acquiring agent payment infrastructure is faster than a startup building agent payment infrastructure and acquiring Stripe's customers. Anthropic building a product feature is faster than a startup building the same feature and acquiring Anthropic's user base. The crowding-out dynamic is real and accelerating.

The exit landscape

Sokolin's framing for where viable exits exist in the current market: M+A from incumbents that have capital and distribution but lack specific technology. Stripe, JP Morgan, PayPal, Ramp, Brex, Visa, and MasterCard are all potential acquirers of well-positioned fintech-adjacent companies. BVNK, Zerohash, and Securitize received M+A interest earlier in 2025.

Standalone IPO remains viable for companies generating real revenue. Coinbase is the reference point for what a standalone crypto-adjacent public company looks like: primarily a transaction revenue story, expensive to hold through low-volume periods, but real.

The robotics footnote

Sokolin draws a direct parallel between the current robotics investment cycle and the augmented/virtual reality cycle of 2018. Same hardware story, same exponential shipment projections, same concentration of capital into a small number of companies. AR/VR peaked at approximately 10 million headsets per year and did not produce the consumer transformation it promised. Robotics shipments today: approximately 5,000 Unitree units in 2024. The gap between narrative and deployment reality deserves tracking.

The investable version of robotics exposure, in his view, is infrastructure: compute, memory, energy, and manufacturing capacity. The same lesson that applied to AI models (invest in the picks and shovels, not the gold rush) applies to physical robots.
